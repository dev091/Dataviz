# First-Year Revenue Model Assumptions

## Planning Rule
These scenarios are illustrative. They are not guarantees and should not be treated as forecasts with certainty.

## Revenue Building Blocks
- annual platform subscription
- onboarding fee where margin-positive
- add-ons for automation, governance, premium connectors, or deployment model

## Scenario Frame

### Learning Scenario
- 10 customers
- average ACV: `$45k`
- average onboarding fee: `$10k`

Illustrative result:
- annualized subscription base: `$450k`
- onboarding revenue: `$100k`

### Strong Execution Scenario
- 25 customers
- average ACV: `$90k`
- average onboarding fee: `$15k`

Illustrative result:
- annualized subscription base: `$2.25M`
- onboarding revenue: `$375k`

### Outstanding Execution Scenario
- 40 customers
- average ACV: `$150k`
- average onboarding fee: `$20k`

Illustrative result:
- annualized subscription base: `$6.0M`
- onboarding revenue: `$800k`

### Exceptional Path Scenario
- 60 customers
- average ACV: `$220k`
- average onboarding fee: `$25k`

Illustrative result:
- annualized subscription base: `$13.2M`
- onboarding revenue: `$1.5M`

## Interpretation
The product and pricing should absolutely be designed for premium enterprise economics. But a direct path to `$20M+` operating profit in the first 12 months is an extremely aggressive target and should not be treated as the planning base case.

The correct operating stance is:
- build for premium ACV
- keep onboarding fast and bounded
- protect gross margin
- enable expansion without heavy services

That creates the preconditions for exceptional economics, even if first-year outcomes are more likely to fall below the most aggressive headline case.
